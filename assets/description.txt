This haiku captures a moment of quiet endurance inside turbulent weather. The opening line, “stirred up by the wind,” suggests something small and vulnerable—perhaps leaves, dust, or even the speaker’s own thoughts—thrown into motion by forces beyond its control. The middle line, “waiting the end of the storm,” shifts from action to stillness: the central mood is not panic but patient suspension, as if the world is holding its breath until the violence passes. The final line, “mingled with the rain,” blends the stirred-up object with the storm itself, implying surrender, cleansing, or transformation—what was separate is now absorbed into a larger cycle. Overall, the poem uses weather as a metaphor for emotional upheaval, emphasizing resilience and the way hardship can dissolve boundaries and reshape what we thought was fixed.
