This haiku sketches a sharp irony: in trying to “avoid remarks” (judgment, criticism, or public scrutiny), the speaker turns to “spent whole days in gossiping,” a social habit that seems protective but is actually self-defeating. The middle line suggests time wasted in idle talk—using other people’s stories as a shield—while the final line, “her stomach empty,” lands as both literal deprivation and moral/emotional hunger. The emptiness implies neglected needs: she feeds on chatter instead of food, purpose, or honest connection. Overall, the poem critiques a life shaped by fear of others’ opinions, where avoidance becomes a kind of self-starvation.
