This haiku blends biblical resonance with domestic satire. The opening phrase, “whose name is legion,” echoes the Gospel story of the possessed man, where “Legion” suggests an overwhelming multitude—here repurposed to imply a swarm of stains, germs, or guilty habits too numerous to name. The second line, “of more frequent washing,” shifts abruptly into the everyday, turning the grand, ominous allusion into a practical remedy: cleanliness as exorcism. The closing line, “you would be quite right,” lands like a dry, courteous concession—an understated punchline that reads as both advice and mild reprimand. Overall, the poem’s wit comes from its contrast: epic language applied to something as ordinary as washing, suggesting that what haunts us may be small, recurring, and best handled through simple, repeated care.
