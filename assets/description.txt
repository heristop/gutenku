This haiku captures a quiet moment of aftermath: something has finished—an argument, a season, a relationship, or even a life chapter—and the world exhales into stillness. The second line introduces “the bird” as a symbol of return, peace, or a long-awaited message, arriving only when the turmoil is done. In the final line, the phrase “with whom they were both” suggests a shared connection: two people, once divided or preoccupied, are united (or reunited) by the same presence—perhaps a memory, a grief, a hope, or a common understanding. The poem’s power lies in its restraint: it doesn’t explain what ended, who “they” are, or what the bird represents, inviting the reader to feel the hush of closure and the subtle reconciliation that follows.
