This haiku turns on a haunting conditional—“but it would”—as if the speaker is imagining a consequence that cannot be fully proved. The phrase “stream in” suggests an ongoing flow, something continuous and unstoppable, while “the fall of heavy bodies” evokes gravity, mortality, and the physical certainty of descent. Yet the final line destabilizes that certainty: “substance or shadow” asks whether what falls is truly material (a body, a fact, a weight) or merely an impression (a silhouette, a memory, a fear). The poem’s power lies in this tension between the measurable and the elusive: it invites us to picture a world where meaning pours in through collapse, where loss arrives like a current, and where we cannot tell if what we witness is real presence or only its dark outline.
