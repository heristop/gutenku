This haiku creates a quiet, tense scene of movement and restraint. The phrase "rising to her feet" suggests a gentle awakening or careful preparation, while "as she went from rock to rock" evokes a difficult, uneven journey, perhaps across a riverbank or shoreline. The final line, "were silent for fear," introduces an atmosphere of anxiety and caution, implying that the speaker or others are keeping still and quiet to avoid danger. The poem’s power lies in its simplicity: the imagery is vivid, but the meaning remains open, allowing readers to sense both physical movement and emotional unease. The silence becomes more than the absence of sound—it becomes a sign of fear, vulnerability, and suspense.
