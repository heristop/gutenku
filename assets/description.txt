This haiku captures a moment of sudden transformation: “soon after rising” suggests a new beginning—morning, awakening, or the start of an emotional shift. The middle line, with its “ebbing and flowing tides,” evokes change as a natural rhythm, hinting at uncertainty, fluctuation, and the pull of opposing forces. Yet the final line—“were solid and firm”—introduces a striking contrast, as if what was once fluid has abruptly settled into certainty. The poem can be read as a meditation on how instability can give way to resolve: feelings steady, decisions harden, or a fragile situation becomes anchored. The tension between water’s motion and the language of solidity creates the haiku’s central effect: the quiet astonishment of finding firmness where one expects continual movement.
