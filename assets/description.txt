This haiku captures the quiet instability of power and loyalty. The opening phrase, "whom it seldom paid," suggests people or institutions that are neglected, unrewarded, or taken for granted—those who serve without receiving due recognition. The second line, "civil and military," widens the scope to society’s twin pillars of authority: governance and force. By ending with "is daily changing," the poem underscores how quickly these structures—and the relationships within them—can rearrange themselves. The effect is slightly ironic and disillusioned: payment, favour, and allegiance are not steady moral currencies but mutable transactions, shifting from day to day. Overall, the haiku reads like a compressed political observation: what seems established is, in fact, constantly in flux.
