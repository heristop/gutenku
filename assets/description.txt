This haiku turns a simple image into something quietly unsettling. The first and third lines repeat “floating on the sea,” creating a circular structure that feels like an endless, inescapable motion—waves returning, or a thought the speaker cannot move past. Between these identical lines, the middle line shifts our attention to the shore “full of people,” setting up a striking contrast: the land is crowded, yet the speaker’s gaze is drawn outward to the sea.

The repetition also blurs boundaries. Are the people merely watching the water, or are they—metaphorically or literally—part of what is floating? The haiku’s ambiguity invites multiple readings: a serene scene of boats on a busy beach, a dreamlike vision where everyone seems adrift, or even a darker suggestion of bodies or lives displaced. With minimal words, it captures a mood of distance and disorientation, where the sea becomes a mirror for collective uncertainty.
