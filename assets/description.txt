This haiku traces a movement from near-death grief to fragile survival. The opening line, “A breath revives him,” suggests a moment of rescue—either literal revival or the return of will after emotional collapse. The second line, “My Garden boasts no flower,” shifts into a stark image of barrenness: the garden becomes a metaphor for the speaker’s inner life, stripped of joy, beauty, and growth. In the final line, “Grief would have killed me,” the poem names the force behind that emptiness, revealing how close sorrow came to destroying the speaker. The tension between “revives” and “would have killed” captures a narrow escape, while the absent flowers imply that recovery is not the same as restoration—life continues, but the landscape remains wounded.
