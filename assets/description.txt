This haiku presents a stark landscape defined by absence: “nor drop of water” opens with a deprivation that feels absolute, immediately establishing drought and longing. The image that follows—“resting on a sea of fire”—turns the ground itself into something volatile and hostile, as if heat has replaced water and the world has been inverted. Finally, “of rocks piled on rocks” sharpens the scene into harsh texture and weight: a barren accumulation with no softness, no shelter, no life-giving moisture. The poem’s power lies in its elemental contrasts—water versus fire, fluidity versus rigidity—and in how it uses minimal language to evoke desolation, endurance, and a quiet warning about environments pushed beyond balance.
