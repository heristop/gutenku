This haiku sketches a quiet scene that feels polite on the surface yet unsettled underneath. The first line—“A man followed her”—drops us into a moment of pursuit, hinting at imbalance or threat without explaining motives. The second line—“After tea will do quite well”—introduces the language of social ritual: tea suggests manners, restraint, and the expectation that things can be smoothed over later, as though discomfort can be postponed or domesticated. The final line—“You are so thoughtful”—lands as ambiguous praise: it may be sincere gratitude, but it can also read as performative courtesy, a mask used to keep peace in an uneasy situation. Overall, the poem contrasts the gentility of etiquette with the tension of being followed, letting omission and understatement create emotional pressure.
