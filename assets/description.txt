This haiku meditates on time, mortality, and the persistence of love. The opening phrase, “which have the like tales,” suggests that human lives repeat familiar patterns—stories of longing, loss, and return that echo across generations. In the central image, “earth passing into the earth,” the poem turns toward the inevitability of death: bodies and all material things dissolve back into their origin, emphasizing the cyclical, unadorned truth of nature. Yet the final line, “Thou light of my heart,” introduces a sudden, intimate address that counters the darkness of decay with inner radiance. The speaker implies that even as everything physical is reclaimed by the earth, there remains a guiding presence—love, memory, or spiritual devotion—that illuminates the self. The haiku’s power lies in this contrast: the vast, impersonal cycle of the natural world set against a singular, personal light that endures.
