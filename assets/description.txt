This haiku traces a quiet transformation from shadow to radiance. The opening simile, “like a bird of night,” evokes secrecy, solitude, and a life adapted to darkness—suggesting a speaker or subject that begins in obscurity. The middle line, “nearly dry in the summer,” introduces a seasonal trial: heat and scarcity threaten vitality, implying emotional or spiritual depletion as much as physical drought. Yet the final line, “bright in blooming charms,” turns decisively toward renewal. “Bright” counters the earlier night imagery, and “blooming” suggests resilience—beauty that emerges not because conditions are easy, but in spite of them. Overall, the poem compresses a narrative of endurance: from nocturnal dimness, through near-withering, into a surprising, luminous flourishing.
