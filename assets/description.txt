This haiku turns on a sharp irony: something that was “before free” becomes constrained “by the same act.” The poem suggests a legal or social gesture—perhaps ownership, marriage, conquest, or a contract—that simultaneously defines both “person and possession.” By pairing these terms, the speaker blurs the boundary between human identity and property, implying that the act meant to order or secure life instead reduces a person to something owned. The word “therefore” gives the line a cold, logical tone, as if the loss of freedom is presented as an inevitable conclusion. Overall, the haiku reads like a compact critique of systems that claim legitimacy while quietly transforming freedom into belonging—of turning a self into a thing.
