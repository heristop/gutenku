This haiku captures the sting of a sudden farewell and the shock of realizing how far something—or someone—has declined. “Fare thee well at once” sounds brisk and final, as if the speaker forces distance to protect themselves. The middle line, “what a falling off was there,” echoes a Shakespearean sense of disillusionment: admiration has collapsed into disappointment, suggesting a betrayal, moral weakening, or the fading of love. The closing line, “No reckoning made,” deepens the hurt; there is no honest accounting, no apology, no explanation—only an ending without closure. Overall, the poem compresses a whole breakup or rupture into three sharp breaths: departure, recognition of loss, and the unresolved silence that follows.
