This haiku turns on repetition and contrast: “winter and summer” frames the poem like two bookends, suggesting the cycle of time and the way opposites recur and depend on each other. Between these extremes sits “a very light mulatto,” an image of in-betweenness—someone read as mixed, liminal, and difficult to place within rigid categories. The adjective “very light” intensifies the sense of ambiguity, implying how perception, identity, and belonging can shift with context, much like temperature and light shift between seasons. By returning to “winter and summer,” the poem implies that the speaker’s experience is caught between poles—cold and heat, exclusion and inclusion, fixed labels and lived complexity—while also hinting at endurance: the seasons keep turning, and the self persists within their changing weather.
