This haiku captures a tense moment where private hardship suddenly meets public authority. The opening line, “which she had in pawn,” suggests quiet desperation: an object of value—perhaps sentimental, perhaps necessary—has been surrendered for survival. The second line shifts the scene into the formal world of judgment, “the justice,” where personal stories are weighed and exposed. In the final line, “the master looked pale,” power visibly falters; the one who should be composed is unsettled, hinting at guilt, recognition, or fear of what will be revealed. The poem’s force lies in what it withholds: we are not told what was pawned or why, but the emotional geometry is clear—poverty, accusation, and authority collide in a single, sharp instant.
