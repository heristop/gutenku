This haiku reads like a fragment of a pledge: something has been “made” with steady purpose, not only in daylight but “by night as well as by day,” suggesting tireless care, vigilance, or service. The shift from the personal (“for which it was made”) to the formal phrase “Along with this grant” introduces a legal or ceremonial tone, as if the speaker is recording a duty attached to a gift, title, or permission. The poem’s power lies in its contrast: the intimate rhythm of constant labor set against the impersonal language of a contract. It hints that true dedication is not occasional or performative—it is continuous, binding, and quietly enduring across time’s two great halves.
