This haiku turns on a quiet sense of bafflement: “How does it happen” opens with a speaker who is genuinely puzzled, inviting us into a moment of inquiry rather than certainty. The phrase “in the title of baron” shifts the focus from a person to a social label—rank, inheritance, and the weight of nobility—suggesting that power can reside as much in a name as in any action. The final line, “have them burnished up,” introduces the central image: burnishing implies polishing metal until it gleams, so the poem hints at reputation-making—how titles, histories, and even people are polished into something impressive. Read together, the haiku can be taken as a critique of status: it wonders how authority becomes accepted and admired, as if it were merely a surface made shiny through ceremony, publicity, or tradition. The spare grammar and slightly askew phrasing add to the effect, mirroring the speaker’s uncertainty and making the “gloss” of nobility feel both constructed and suspect.
