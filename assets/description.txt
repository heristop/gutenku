This haiku opens with a quiet downward motion—“and the rain descends”—setting a gentle, reflective tone and grounding us in sound and sensation. The middle line, “the brief bright night of summer,” highlights summer’s fleeting intensity: a season of light that feels abundant yet passes quickly. The final image, “where the drifting snow,” introduces a surprising seasonal shift, like a memory or a dream intruding on the present. Read as juxtaposition, the poem layers rain, summer brightness, and snow to suggest how time and feeling overlap: warmth can contain the echo of cold, and a luminous moment can be shadowed by what came before (or what will return). The haiku’s power lies in this delicate contradiction, inviting us to experience weather as metaphor—an emotional landscape where transience, nostalgia, and quiet wonder meet.
