This haiku offers a wry, almost clinical observation about experience. The opening phrase, “Taken as a whole,” suggests a detached step back—as if life, a day, or even a person can be surveyed like a completed report. “Account for it as we may” deepens that sense of analysis: we try to explain, justify, or rationalize what we’re seeing, searching for meaning through logic and interpretation. Yet the final line, “involves boredom,” undercuts those efforts with an anticlimax. The poem implies that even our grand attempts to total up existence can lead to a plain, deflating truth: monotony is built into the human condition.

The haiku’s humour lies in its deflation and its bureaucratic diction—“account for it” sounds like bookkeeping, making boredom feel like an unavoidable item on the ledger of life. Structurally, the last line acts as a punchline, shifting the tone from philosophical to dryly candid. Overall, the poem captures how over-explaining or over-summarising life can flatten it, leaving us with the very dullness we were trying to escape.
