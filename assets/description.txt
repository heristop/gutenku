This haiku captures a quiet moment of wonder, filtered through nighttime perception. The opening phrase, “birds and images,” juxtaposes the natural with the imagined or remembered, suggesting that what he sees may be both real and symbolic—birds as living presences, “images” as mental pictures, shadows, or dreams. The middle line, “How curious it all seemed,” places us inside the observer’s reflective mind: the tone is not fearful but intrigued, as if the ordinary world has turned slightly strange under the night’s influence. The final line, “He saw them at night,” shifts the scene into darkness, where vision is uncertain and meaning becomes more suggestive than definite. Overall, the poem explores how night alters perception, making familiar things—like birds—feel mysterious, and blurring the boundary between external sight and inner imagination.
