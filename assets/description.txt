This haiku turns on a quiet shock: the speaker’s plain confession—“was a married man”—immediately casts a moral and emotional shadow over what follows. The second line, “so the cradles were empty,” works as both literal image and symbolic consequence. A cradle suggests infancy, continuity, and sanctioned family life; its emptiness implies absence, loss, or a life that cannot openly begin. The logic of “so” makes the emptiness feel like an outcome of the first line, hinting at secrecy, divided loyalties, or choices that foreclose new beginnings.

The final line, “Roxana by name,” narrows the poem from circumstance to a single person, giving the implied story a human face. Naming her arrives like a belated acknowledgement—tender, guilty, or elegiac—suggesting that Roxana exists in the margins of the married man’s life, remembered but not fully claimed. The haiku’s restraint is its power: it offers no explicit scandal, only a spare chain of cause, image, and name, leaving the reader to feel the weight of what is unsaid.
