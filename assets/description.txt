This haiku captures a sudden rush from motion into fate. The opening command—“put spurs to his horse”—signals urgency and decisive action, as if the rider is compelled to move before doubt can intervene. The second line’s simile, “as white as the driven snow,” paints the horse with a stark, almost symbolic purity: whiteness suggests innocence, clarity, or an ideal the rider believes in. Yet the final line—“the dice were produced”—shifts the scene from heroic momentum to the cold mechanics of chance. What seemed like controlled action becomes a gamble, implying that the rider’s charge may be less about mastery and more about risking everything on uncertain outcomes. The haiku’s power lies in this abrupt turn: speed and whiteness set up a romantic image, only to be undercut by the revelation that fate, not valor, may decide the result.
