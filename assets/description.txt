This haiku captures a sudden, unsettling discovery: a husband is found with “Filippa,” and the final line—“lover of hers”—twists the situation into a knot of betrayal and irony. The poem’s power lies in its fragmentary, unfinished feel; the syntax is deliberately awkward, as if the speaker can barely form the sentence while processing shock. “Her husband” establishes a domestic bond, “Filippa” introduces a specific, human presence (a name that makes the betrayal feel real rather than abstract), and the closing phrase suggests a layered triangle: the woman’s husband is caught with someone who is, in fact, the woman’s own lover. The haiku’s compression mirrors the emotional compression of the moment—confusion, exposure, and the collapse of private relationships into public revelation. The trailing “and” leaves the scene unresolved, implying consequences still unfolding beyond the poem’s frame.
