This haiku pivots on a quiet shock: the speaker states he “was a married man,” a phrase that usually implies stability, legacy, and children—yet the next line, “so the cradles were empty,” overturns that expectation with a stark image of absence. The cradles suggest not only literal childlessness but also a hollowed domestic life, a marriage that has failed to become a home in the fullest sense.

The final line, “Roxana by name,” lands like a confession or identification of the missing presence. Naming Roxana personalizes the emptiness: she may be a wife, a lover, or a figure associated with loss, betrayal, or a life that never arrived. The haiku’s power lies in its compression—three short lines hint at an entire narrative of marriage, consequence, and longing, leaving the reader to infer whether the emptiness is caused by choice, tragedy, or emotional distance.
