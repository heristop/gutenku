This haiku contrasts abundance with decay. The opening phrase, "heated with the feast," suggests a moment of lively consumption, warmth, and celebration. But this energy is sharply interrupted by "an old worm-eaten patent," which introduces age, damage, and neglect. The final line, "How many are there," turns the poem into a quiet question of uncertainty, as if the speaker is counting or noticing something overlooked. Together, the lines create a subtle tension between richness and ruin, reminding us that even what seems valuable or complete may be worn away by time.
