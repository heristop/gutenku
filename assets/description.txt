This haiku juxtaposes nature’s beauty with the brutal cost of human conflict. The opening image, “the living sea rose,” suggests a sea that is animate—breathing, swelling, and almost sentient—its movement both majestic and ominous. The second line, “made by the falling wounded,” sharply reframes that natural surge as something created by violence: the “rising” becomes a metaphorical tide of blood, bodies, and loss, implying that the sea’s motion is inseparable from the suffering that feeds it. The final line, “the massive stone walls,” introduces an immovable man-made presence—fortifications, coastlines of defense, or barriers of power—against which this living, tragic tide breaks. Structurally, the poem moves from fluidity (sea) to human consequence (wounded) to hardness (stone), emphasizing the contrast between what yields and what resists, and leaving the reader with a haunting sense of endurance: nature continues, walls remain, but the wounded fall in between.
