This haiku places us “far back from the road,” away from traffic, noise, and human hurry—suggesting a secluded vantage point where reflection becomes possible. The second line, “The sun had set some time since,” shifts the scene into aftermath: not the dramatic moment of sunset, but the quiet interval that follows, when light has thinned and the world feels slightly suspended. The final phrase, “eager and wistful,” introduces a gentle emotional contradiction. “Eager” implies forward-looking anticipation, while “wistful” carries longing for what has already passed. Together, they capture twilight as a state of in-between: the day is gone, yet the speaker’s feelings remain alert, tender, and expectant—poised between memory and desire.
