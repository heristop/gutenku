This haiku sketches a quietly comic domestic scene through the lens of reputation and appetite. The opening phrase, “a famous scholar,” establishes a figure of public esteem—someone associated with learning, discipline, and refinement. The second line shifts the focus from status to story: “The cousin arrived at last” suggests anticipation, delay, and the small drama of a long-awaited visitor finally appearing. In the final line, “likewise well filled,” the poem gently undercuts the grandeur of scholarship by placing the scholar and the cousin on equal, human terms—both are “filled,” implying satisfaction after a meal, or more broadly a sense of being content, complete, and comfortably provided for. The word “likewise” is key: it levels hierarchy, hinting that beneath titles and expectations, people share the same ordinary needs and pleasures. Overall, the haiku balances respect with irony, turning a moment of arrival into an observation about how intellect and kinship meet in the everyday rituals of hospitality.
