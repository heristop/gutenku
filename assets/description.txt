This haiku captures a tender contradiction: someone appears “very pale,” suggesting illness, exhaustion, fear, or emotional fragility, yet a “ripple of laughter” breaks through that pallor like light on water. The word “ripple” is crucial—it implies something small but spreading, a gentle movement that changes the whole surface. In the final line, “is still more to me,” the speaker quietly reveals their values: even a faint, fleeting laugh outweighs the visible signs of suffering. The poem therefore becomes an intimate statement of devotion and attention—finding meaning not in perfect health or appearances, but in a moment of shared humanity that endures in the speaker’s heart.
