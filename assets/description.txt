This haiku captures a stark reversal of power. The first line, "his gun on the ground," signals sudden disarmament—whether through defeat, surrender, or shock—so the weapon becomes a symbol of lost control. The second line, "uttering pitiful cries," shifts the focus from violence to vulnerability, exposing a raw, almost childlike fear that contrasts with the implied threat of the gun. The final line, "The hideous creature," delivers a chilling ambiguity: it may name an external monster, but it can also be read as a judgment on the crying figure himself, suggesting that ugliness is moral as much as physical. Overall, the poem compresses a moment of collapse into three sharp images, forcing the reader to question who the real monster is and how quickly strength can turn into humiliation.
