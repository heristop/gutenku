This haiku speaks in a colloquial, dialect voice—“en dey wuz”—which immediately places us in a lived, remembered scene rather than a polished, distant narration. The opening question, “what did come of it,” suggests reflection after the fact: the speaker is weighing outcomes, asking what remains when an event, a season, or even a relationship has passed.

The middle line, “en dey wuz a good current,” introduces motion and energy. “Current” can be read literally (a stream or river) and metaphorically (a strong flow of feeling, time, or change). Calling it “good” implies it carried something forward—life-giving, cleansing, or promising.

The final line, “and green summer grapes,” delivers the sensory image typical of haiku: vivid, immediate, and seasonal. “Green” grapes are often unripe—tart, not yet sweet—so the ending holds a gentle tension between abundance and incompletion. Summer suggests fullness and warmth, yet the grapes’ greenness hints that what “came of it” is still developing. Overall, the poem balances movement (the current) with stillness (fruit on the vine), capturing a moment where the speaker finds meaning not in a grand conclusion, but in what the season has quietly produced.
