This haiku presents a bleak miniature of power and blame. The opening clause, “If others hanged him,” suggests a conditional, almost offhand account of execution—implying that responsibility is dispersed (“others”), while the victim is singular (“him”). The second line, “a modified monarchy,” shifts from the personal to the political: not a revolution, but an adjusted system that keeps the crown’s logic intact while changing its outward form. In the final image, “on a straw shake-down,” the poem lands on something both flimsy and coercive—straw evokes poverty, makeshift bedding, or a scarecrow’s emptiness, while “shake-down” implies extortion or a forced search. Together, the haiku critiques how regimes—especially those only ‘modified’—maintain control by staging punishments and extracting compliance, all on a foundation as insubstantial as straw.
