This haiku captures a moment of emotional distance: the speaker deliberately refuses to meet “him,” even as his voice betrays vulnerability. The first line, “not looking at him,” signals avoidance—an act of self-protection or quiet defiance. The second line, “with a quiver in his voice,” introduces a crack in his composure, suggesting fear, grief, or pleading, and invites empathy. Yet the final line—“what is that to me”—cuts sharply against that invitation. It reads as dismissal, numbness, or a practiced indifference, implying a history that has exhausted the speaker’s capacity to care. The poem’s power lies in its restraint: in three brief lines, it stages a conflict between another person’s exposed emotion and the speaker’s refusal to be moved.
