This haiku sketches a stark portrait of isolation and guilt. The opening phrase, “as far by water,” places the man at a distance—separated not only by physical space but by an emotional barrier the water symbolises: a boundary that is fluid, reflective, and hard to cross. In the second line, “self-condemning is his look,” the poem turns inward; the harshest judgment comes from within, and his expression becomes a kind of confession. The final line, “Miserable man,” delivers a blunt, unadorned conclusion that feels both compassionate and fatalistic, as if the speaker can do nothing but name what they see. Overall, the haiku compresses a whole tragedy into a single image: a solitary figure, mirrored by water, trapped in remorse and estrangement.
