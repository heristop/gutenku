This haiku sets its scene at daybreak, a moment that typically suggests renewal, yet it immediately subverts that expectation by presenting an emptiness at the heart of the morning. The phrase “which happened to be empty” reads like a quiet, almost offhand revelation, but it carries weight: the speaker confronts a space—literal or emotional—where comfort or meaning should be. The final line, “still trembling with fright,” introduces a lingering aftershock, implying that whatever caused the fear occurred before dawn and has not been dispelled by the coming light. The poem’s power lies in its tension between the promise of a new day and the persistence of anxiety, suggesting that some terrors remain even when darkness lifts.
