This haiku moves in three swift, emotionally charged steps. The first line, “toward a high elm,” lifts the scene upward and outward: the elm suggests age, height, and a watchful natural presence, as if the speaker’s feelings are being carried toward something larger than human life. The second line—“and he was sorry for her”—drops us into a private moral moment. The pronoun shift to “he” creates distance, like a remembered story or a confession told indirectly, and the sorrow implies an imbalance of power, a wound, or a failure to protect.

The final line, “since ye know my name,” introduces an archaic, almost biblical diction (“ye”), which turns the poem into an address—part plea, part justification. Knowing someone’s name implies intimacy and accountability: once the speaker is recognized, he cannot hide behind anonymity. Taken together, the haiku reads like a fragment of a larger narrative: nature as witness, remorse as the turning point, and naming as the moment of reckoning. The poem’s power lies in what it withholds; the gaps invite us to imagine the untold circumstances that make the apology necessary.
