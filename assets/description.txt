This haiku captures a moment of quiet self-correction. The speaker “look[s] round the room,” suggesting an ordinary interior space observed with a critical eye. The second line—“uncouth is too hard a word”—reveals a pause in judgment: the speaker recognizes that calling what they see “uncouth” (crude, awkward, unrefined) is too harsh, too absolute. In the final line, “as some tree or cloud,” the poem shifts into a gentler comparison with nature. Like a tree’s irregular branches or a cloud’s changing shape, the room (or the people/things in it) may be imperfect yet still natural, expressive, and worthy of patient attention. The haiku’s movement from scrutiny to compassion suggests that roughness can be a kind of organic authenticity, and that language itself can either wound or soften what we perceive.
