This haiku sketches a quiet drama of desire and compromise. The opening, “finding it locked,” suggests an emotional door—trust, intimacy, or a relationship—discovered to be inaccessible. In response, “adopting the substitute” implies a reluctant replacement: choosing a second-best comfort, a stand-in affection, or a coping mechanism when the true object of longing cannot be reached. The final phrase, “the captive lover,” twists the scene inward; the lover is not only confined by circumstance but also by their own choices, settling for substitution while remaining emotionally imprisoned. The haiku’s power lies in its ambiguity: the lock may be external (social barriers, rejection) or internal (fear, guardedness), yet the outcome is the same—love constrained, redirected, and quietly trapped.
