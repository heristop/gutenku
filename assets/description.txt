This haiku paints a spare, atmospheric landscape where mood is carried by setting rather than action. “In the evening” establishes a liminal time—day fading into night—often associated with reflection and quiet unease. The phrase “such dreary mountains” suggests more than physical bleakness; it projects an emotional heaviness onto the natural world, as if the speaker’s inner state is echoed by the terrain. The final line, “dusky and silent,” deepens the stillness: light drains away, sound seems to vanish, and the mountains become indistinct shapes, amplifying a sense of isolation. Overall, the poem uses minimal imagery to create a powerful tone of melancholy and contemplative calm, inviting the reader to feel the hush and weight of twilight in a desolate place.
