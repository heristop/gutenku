This haiku stages a quiet crisis: a speaker facing darkness without the usual protections. “the blade not forged” suggests more than the absence of a weapon—it implies unreadiness, a self not yet tempered by experience, discipline, or resolve. The second line, “The night had come on apace,” accelerates the threat: night arrives swiftly, bringing uncertainty and the sense of being overtaken. In the final question, “How does one kill fear,” the poem pivots from external danger to an inner adversary. The verb “kill” is strikingly violent, especially when no blade exists; it hints that fear cannot be defeated by force or tools alone. Instead, the haiku implies a different kind of forging—courage shaped in the moment, acceptance of vulnerability, or a mental steadiness that must be crafted when time is short and darkness is already here.
