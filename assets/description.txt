This haiku captures the quiet, almost imperceptible movement of night as it “creeps so slowly,” suggesting time unfolding with hushed patience. The moon’s light “shines in all around,” filling the landscape not with heat or force but with a gentle, enveloping radiance. In the final line—“just as the sun does”—the poet draws an elegant comparison between moon and sun, implying a shared authority: both illuminate, both shape our perception, and both govern cycles of living. The effect is contemplative, inviting us to notice how different kinds of light can feel—one bold and immediate, the other subtle and gradual—yet each is complete in its own way.
