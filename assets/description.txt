This haiku captures a fleeting moment of wonder, as if we are arriving mid-story. The opening line, “Rose was astonished,” suggests both a character named Rose and the flower’s symbolic presence—beauty meeting surprise. The second line, “and again the magic worked,” implies repetition: whatever spell, charm, or hopeful attempt has succeeded before succeeds once more, giving the scene a quiet sense of reassurance and inevitability. The final line, “Probably they were,” is intriguingly unfinished; it trails off into uncertainty, like a thought interrupted. That ambiguity invites the reader to supply what “they” might be—believers, transformed, together, forgiven—turning the poem into a small mystery. Overall, the haiku balances enchantment with doubt, showing how magic can feel real in the instant it happens, even if certainty slips away immediately after.
