This haiku creates a haunting image of devastation and lingering heat. The phrase "which was still on fire" suggests that something terrible has recently happened, leaving behind destruction and danger. The line "The houses seemed deserted" adds a sense of emptiness and loss, as if the people have fled or been driven away. The repetition of "which was still on fire" at the end intensifies the feeling of shock and unfinished disaster, making the scene feel frozen in a moment of aftermath. The poem is brief, but it powerfully conveys ruin, silence, and the unsettling persistence of fire.
