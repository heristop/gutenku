This haiku sets up a quiet contrast between two worlds: the familiar side governed by rules and compliance, and an “other side” where the speaker imagines greater freedom. The phrase “requires less obedience” suggests that conventional education can feel like training in conformity—learning to follow instructions as much as to think. Yet the final line, “though education,” complicates the escape: even on that freer side, learning still exists, but it may be self-directed, experiential, or rooted in curiosity rather than discipline. The haiku’s slight grammatical friction mirrors its theme—pushing against tidy structures, including the structures of schooling and authority.
